
### Anology 

Trust is like glass — once cracked, it may still hold, but it will never look the same again

Source Model (Glass)

Entities:
- Glass
- Crack
- External Force

Attributes:
- Glass has a state: intact or cracked
- Glass has structural strength
- Glass has visual appearance
- Glass has functional capability (ability to hold or support)

Relationships:
- External force causes a crack in the glass
- A crack changes the state of the glass from intact to damaged
- A crack alters the visual appearance of the glass
- A crack may reduce structural strength but does not necessarily eliminate functionality
- Glass can still perform its function even when cracked

Constraints:
- The damage (crack) is irreversible
- The original appearance of the glass cannot be fully restored
- Structural integrity may be permanently weakened

### Now for source model - Trust

Entities - 
- Trust
- Breach
- Betrayal

Attributes
- Trust is built over time.
- As time progresses and bonds become closer trust becomes more prevalent.
- Trust is an emotion / feeling rather than being visible. It is an abstract entity.
- Trust holds frienships/ relationships/ bonds together

Relationships - 
- Betrayal causes a breach of trust.
- Lack of trust leads to broken frienships/ relationships.
- Lack of Trust changes the dynamic of a relationship. It leads to sadness and unhappiness.
- Lack of trust might not break a freinship completely, but it does affect the bond. The bond will never be as strong as it used to be.
- People who do not fully trust each other can still be friends, but there will always be a sense of wariness because of past distrustful experiences.

Constraints -
- Betrayal of trust is irreversible
- Original bond will not be fully restored/ takes a lot of time and effort to go back to the same relationship. People do not forget and forgive as easily as it is said.
- The bond might be weak forever even though people are friends.

## Polished version

Entities:
- Trust
- Betrayal
- Breach of trust
- Individual (actor causing betrayal)

Attributes:
- Trust is an abstract, non-visible entity
- Trust develops over time through interactions
- Trust has strength (strong or weak)
- Trust supports relationships and bonds between individuals

Relationships:
- An individual’s actions can cause betrayal
- Betrayal leads to a breach of trust
- A breach weakens the strength of trust in a relationship
- Reduced trust alters the dynamics of the relationship
- Relationships may continue to exist even with weakened trust

Constraints:
- A breach of trust is difficult to fully reverse
- The original strength of trust is rarely completely restored
- Past betrayal continues to influence future interactions

This analogy highlights the structural similarities between physical damage in glass and emotional damage in trust. In both cases, an external force or action causes a transition from an intact state to a damaged one. Just as cracked glass loses its original strength but may still function, a relationship can continue even after trust is broken, though in a weakened form. Additionally, the crack alters the appearance of the glass, similar to how betrayal changes the perception of trust between individuals. Finally, the analogy emphasizes irreversibility, as both cracked glass and broken trust are difficult to fully restore to their original state.

## Second anology

While the original analogy compares trust to glass and emphasizes fragility, damage, and irreversibility, the revised analogy compares trust to a flame and highlights its dynamic and controllable nature. In the glass analogy, trust is portrayed as something that, once broken, is permanently altered and difficult to restore. In contrast, the flame analogy suggests that trust depends on how it is managed over time. A controlled flame provides warmth and stability, similar to how trust, when built gradually and handled with care, strengthens relationships. However, an uncontrolled flame can cause harm, just as misplaced or excessive trust can lead to negative consequences. Thus, while the original analogy presents trust as passively damaged, the revised analogy reframes it as an active process that requires careful handling.
